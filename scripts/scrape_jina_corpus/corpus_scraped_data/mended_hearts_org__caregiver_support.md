C O N N E C T W I T H O T H E R S W H O U N D E R S T A N D Sometimes we can feel like we are the only person having the feelings we have, and we can be embarrassed or ashamed to admit them. When you connect with others who have gone through 

something similar, you will find that many of the feelings you have others have too. Find others who help you feel supported. Good support groups will provide positive support, even though you may be discussing difficult feelings and emotions. 

W O R K I N G W I T H E M O T I O N S 

B E P R E S E N T The best way to manage emotions, especially fear and stress, is to become present. When you 

find yourself thinking about the future, focus on here and now instead. Become aware of your 

surroundings — who you are with, what you are doing, sounds, smells, etc. The instant the “what 

if…?” thoughts start, take some deep breaths and come back to the present. The truth is, we 

don’t know what the future holds for any of us, so there is no need to fear tomorrow today. 

P R A C T I C E G R A T I T U D E We know it can be very, very hard to feel grateful for anything when you have a child with a 

serious illness, especially during a difficult hospital stay or recovery. Start simple: Each day, list one thing in your life that you are grateful for — even something small like the sunshine outside or a kind gesture from a stranger. Gratitude helps the healing process and turns your focus toward something positive. 

A C K N O W L E D G E Y O U R F E E L I N G S 

Sometimes just being aware of your feelings helps. Trying to fight, avoid or deny your feelings 

may only make things worse and then make you feel guilty about feeling that way. Know that it is okay to feel sad, disappointed, angry, and any other feelings. It does not mean that you don’t love your child. You may find that these feelings will come and go, and your goal is not to get stuck in them. 

W R I T E Y O U R F E E L I N G S D O W N Writing or typing your feelings can help you identify what you are feeling and lessen negative 

impact. Don’t be afraid to write everything you feel, even emotions you may not want to admit you have. This is just for you. It’s not necessary to go back and read what you wrote, just get your emotions out as a means of letting them go.

Some common feelings are grief, fear, anger, guilt, overwhelm, and confusion. You may also experience symptoms of post traumatic stress disorder (PTSD). S T R E T C H A N D E X E R C I S E When you are under a lot of stress, your muscles will get tense. Gentle stretching is a good 

way to release tension. Tensing and then relaxing muscle groups in parts of your body can help reduce tension too. Exercise also helps reduce stress by releasing endorphins which are hormones that can reduce pain and stress and improve your mood. If you can, take a walk outside. There are benefits of being in nature in addition to benefits from exercising. 

D O R E L A X A T I O N E X E R C I S E S 

There are many breathing exercises and imaging exercises that can help reduce your stress 

level. Some breathing exercises, such as box breathing, are even used by people in the military who face stressful situations. Imagining a place or something that makes you feel calm can help too. Meditation is a type of relaxation exercise and a wonderful way to relax that has many scientifically proven health benefits. Find exercises that feel right for you. There are many apps now with relaxation exercises on them as well. 

G E T H E L P When you feel so much stress that you are overwhelmed, get help. Seeing a therapist and/or 

taking medication may make a positive difference in how you feel and your ability to cope. Getting the help you need can be the best thing you can do for you and your child. 

L A U G H The saying “laughter is the best medicine” is true. Laughter reduces stress hormones and can make you feel better. Find videos, memes, TV shows, books, or movies that make you laugh. 

Spend time with people who make you laugh and help brighten your mood. 

G E T A N D S T A Y O R G A N I Z E D When you have a child with health issues, there are often many appointments, medications, 

and other things to keep track of, and this can feel stressful. Being disorganized in general can cause anxiety, especially when there are many things going on at once. As much as possible, get and stay organized. Use calendars, apps, and organizational tools to help you. Learn organizational tricks from other parents too. 

P R A C T I C E M I N D F U L N E S S Mindfulness is when you are totally present in mind and body and paying full attention to what 

you are doing. When you are mindful, you are not worrying about the future or stuck thinking about the past. You can be mindful doing everyday things like drinking a cup or tea or coffee. Use all your senses—taste, smell, feel, look and sound. Mindfulness can help you feel calm.

or a medical procedure, it is likely you are feeling at least a little stress. Studies now show that having a child with a heart problem can have a significant emotional impact on parents. T A K E C A R E O F Y O U R O W N H E A L T H When you are caring for a child with a heart condition, it’s easy to overlook or ignore your own health. But if you aren’t healthy, it will be more difficult to take care of your child.  Make the time to see your doctor or health care provider regularly. Eat healthy meals, get enough sleep, 

get some physical activity, and find tools to reduce stress. If you have recently given birth, it is important to follow your doctor’s recommendations and to keep follow-up appointments. 

A S K F O R A N D A C C E P T H E L P 

Asking for and accepting help can be difficult, but often family and friends  want  to help and 

support you during stressful times, so let them do so. Make a list of things that need to be done and be realistic about what you have the time and energy to do yourself. Keep the list handy, and next time a friend or family member asks how they can help, pick something from it. 

G I V E Y O U R S E L F A B R E A K You don't need to have all the answers or be with your child every second of every day. You need to take breaks! It can be scary to leave your child’s side, particularly after surgery or a 

hospital stay, but you need to get away — even for just a few minutes. Schedule time away from the responsibilities of parenting and caregiving. Make plans for someone you trust to spend 

time with your child while you read a book, take a walk, enjoy a healthy meal, nap...anything that is truly relaxing. 

C O N N E C T W I T H O T H E R S Being the caregiver of a child with medical needs can make you feel isolated and alone. It is 

important to reach out and connect with others who can help you feel supported. Ask your health care provider or social worker to refer you to community support groups or put you in touch with another parent. Use the internet to keep family and friends updated on your child’s condition, care plan, and your needs. Learn about community support services in your area such as social workers, counseling services, pastoral care and other ways to get support. 

T A K E Y O U R F A M I L Y B A C K Don’t let your child’s diagnosis define your family. Recognize that every member of the family may be under increased stress, and this can make it more difficult for you to support each 

other.  This is especially true for siblings who may have fears and anxiety that they are not voicing. It is important for your family to spend time together that is not focused on your child’s 

diagnosis. Plan family game nights, share special meals or attend a social event as a family. 

C A R I N G F O R Y O U R S E L F 

If you feel frustrated, overwhelmed or are unable to manage, call your child’s doctor so he or she can help you find 

support. Don't be afraid to get help if you need it. If you need more information on caring for yourself and your child, 

visit  www.mendedlittlehearts.org .If you have a child who was born with a heart condition, you may think that taking care of your child is 

all that matters. However, you must also take care of yourself so you can effectively care for your child. You will need to learn many new things about caring for your child and make important choices about your child’s care. It's hard to do this well if you’re too tired, physically unwell, overwhelmed, or have high levels of stress or anxiety. That’s why it’s vital for you to learn to take care of yourself too.
