Dear Friend
Health care professionals and parents worked together to create the Mended Little
HeartGuide, so it not only has accurate information but also empowers families to cope
— and even thrive — in a world that can be frightening and difficult. This guide is a
companion to the medical information you’ll receive from your child’s physicians. It
provides answers to many questions you may have about caring for your child, yourself
and the rest of your family.

When your child is diagnosed with a congenital heart
defect (CHD), you may experience many emotions,
especially if your child needs surgery or a medical
procedure. These emotions often come and go
throughout your child’s life.
After a CHD diagnosis, you must suddenly learn how
to care for a child with special medical needs in
addition to learning basic parenting skills. You have
been thrust into a world of surgeries, procedures,
specialists, medications, devices and medical jargon.
You must also accept that CHD is a lifelong
condition requiring specialized care. This can feel
scary and overwhelming.
If your child is doing well, you may feel joy, relief and
gratitude. But even if things are going well, and
especially if they are not, you may also have feelings
of sadness, anger, fear, guilt and confusion, among
other emotions. It can be difficult to work through
these emotions. However, although it may seem
hard to imagine, many parents and caregivers report
that raising a child with CHD has made them
stronger and that they became a kinder and more
compassionate person when they learned to accept
and manage their emotions. 
This section discusses some common thoughts and
feelings that you may be experiencing as a parent or
caregiver of a child with CHD and provides tips on
effective ways to cope with those feelings.
LOSS
It is completely normal for parents and caregivers to
feel grief, and even intense sadness, when their
child is diagnosed with CHD, hospitalized or needs
surgery. Grief is often strongest in times of crisis but
can pop up—sometimes unexpectedly—at other
times during a child’s life as well.
Grief isn’t limited to your child’s initial diagnosis and
a hospital stay. When you get home from the
hospital, grief that you didn’t allow yourself to feel
while you were there may catch up to you. Grief may
show up on milestones like birthdays or the
anniversary of a surgery or diagnosis. Cardiology
appointments, hospital visits and medical procedures
may bring grief back as well. This grief can take
parents and caregivers by surprise when it shows up
well after diagnosis and/or hospitalization because
they thought that they had already worked through
the sadness.
Sometimes parents and caregivers don’t even
understand why they are sad. Here are some
common reasons for grief that can be hard to identify
until you learn about them from another parent:
GRIEF
When discussing the gender of a child before birth,
many parents say, “I don’t care if it’s a boy or a girl,
as long as the baby is healthy.” After a CHD
diagnosis, it is normal to mourn the “healthy baby”
you had hoped to have. This doesn’t mean that you
don’t love your child; you are simply adjusting to
having a child who may face unexpected challenges
and limitations.

While it may seem that your emotions are in control of you and you can’t do anything about them, it will get
better. Be patient with yourself because healing will take time. Here are some specific things you can do to
help you work through feelings:
Sometimes just being aware of your feelings helps.
Trying to fight, avoid or deny your feelings may only
make things worse. Know that it is okay to feel sad,
disappointed, angry and other emotions that come
up. It does not mean that you don’t love your child.
To acknowledge fear, ask yourself, “What am I really
afraid of?” Be specific. Writing your fears down helps
you define and face them. Allow yourself to
experience all your feelings, even guilt and shame,
so you can work through them. Just remember that
your goal is to acknowledge them and move out of
them rather than getting stuck in them.

STAY IN THE PRESENT MOMENT

denying feelings; it means making decisions about
how we

how we will handle them after we acknowledge

One of the best ways to manage emotions,
especially fear and stress, is to become present.
When you find yourself thinking about tomorrow,
focus on right here, right now instead. Become
aware of your surroundings—who you are with, what
you are doing, sounds, smells, etc. Bringing yourself
back to the present will help lessen your fear and
reduce the hold strong emotions have on you. The
instant the “what if …?” thoughts start, take some
deep breaths and come back to the here and now.
The truth is, we don’t know what the future holds for
any of us, so there is no need to live in fear,
sadness, anger, etc. today.

Meeting others like you can give you hope,
especially when you are able to get to know others

managing well. Support groups allow you to talk with
others who truly understand what you’re going
through. 
Getting to know others like you can reduce anger
too. When you can talk honestly about feelings of
resentment towards family and friends who just can’t
seem to understand and toward other parents with
healthy children who don’t realize how lucky they
are, you lessen anger’s hold on you.
If you can, join a reputable support network that can

with parents who have had similar experiences. Be

may be discussing negative feelings and emotions. 
If there is a Mended Little Hearts® group near you,

The safety instructions on airplanes inform you to put
your own oxygen mask on before helping others. If
you need an oxygen mask in a plane, it is because
there is a crisis. The reason why you need to put
your own oxygen mask on first in a crisis is because
you are better able to help others with theirs if you
have yours on.
This same concept is true of caring for a child who
has CHD. It is much more difficult to care for your
child when you have not taken care of yourself--if
you are too tired, physically unwell, overwhelmed, or
have high levels of stress or anxiety. 
Here is some helpful advice from parents of children
with CHD on taking care of yourself:
When you are caring for a child with a heart
condition, it’s easy to overlook or ignore your own
health. But if you aren’t healthy, it will be more
difficult to take care of your child. Make the time to
see your doctor or health care provider regularly. Eat
healthy meals, get enough sleep, get some physical
activity, and find tools to reduce stress. If you have
recently given birth, it is important to follow your
doctor’s recommendations and to keep follow-up
appointments.
ASK FOR AND ACCEPT HELP
Asking for and accepting help can be difficult, but
often family and friends want to help and support
you during stressful times, so let them do so. Make a
list of things that need to be done and be realistic
about what you have the time and energy to do
yourself. Keep the list handy, and next time a friend
or family member asks how they can help, pick
something from it.
GIVE YOURSELF A BREAK
You don't need to have all the answers or be with
your child every second of every day. You need to
take breaks! It can be scary to leave your child’s
side, particularly after surgery or a hospital stay, but
you need to get away— even for just a few minutes.
Schedule time away from the responsibilities of
parenting and caregiving. Make plans for someone
you trust to spend time with your child while you read
a book, take a walk, enjoy a healthy meal,
nap...anything that is truly relaxing.
